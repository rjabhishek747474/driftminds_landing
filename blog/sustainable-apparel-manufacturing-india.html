Sustainable & Ethical Apparel Manufacturing in India – What Brands Need to Know

Drift Minds Team May 5, 2026 11 min read

India's textile and apparel industry is the world's second-largest, contributing over $40 billion in exports annually. As global consumers and brands increasingly prioritize sustainability, Indian manufacturers are at the forefront of the green revolution in fashion. This comprehensive guide explores what sustainable apparel manufacturing means, the certifications that matter, eco-friendly fabric choices, and how brands can make ethical sourcing decisions.

The Rise of Sustainable Fashion in India

The fashion industry is one of the most polluting industries globally, responsible for approximately 10% of global carbon emissions and nearly 20% of wastewater. India, being a major hub for textile production, has both a responsibility and an opportunity to lead the shift toward sustainability.

In recent years, consumer awareness about environmental and social impacts of clothing has surged. A 2025 Nielsen study found that 73% of global consumers would change their consumption habits to reduce environmental impact. This shift has pushed brands — from fast fashion giants to independent labels — to seek out manufacturers who prioritize sustainable practices.

India is uniquely positioned to lead this transformation. The country has a rich tradition of handloom and natural dyeing techniques, abundant cotton production (the world's largest), and a growing ecosystem of certified sustainable factories. States like Tamil Nadu, Gujarat, Maharashtra, Karnataka, and increasingly Bihar and West Bengal are emerging as centers for ethical manufacturing.

What Makes Apparel Manufacturing "Sustainable"?

Sustainable apparel manufacturing encompasses every stage of the production lifecycle — from raw material sourcing to finished garment delivery. Here are the key pillars:

1. Eco-Friendly Raw Materials

Using organic cotton, recycled polyester, bamboo fiber, hemp, and Tencel instead of conventional materials that require heavy pesticide use and water consumption.

2. Water Conservation

Implementing water recycling systems, using waterless dyeing technologies, and choosing fabrics that require less water during cultivation and processing.

3. Energy Efficiency

Adopting solar power, energy-efficient machinery, LED lighting, and process optimizations to minimize carbon footprint during production.

4. Fair Labor Practices

Ensuring fair wages, safe working conditions, no child labor, reasonable working hours, and employee welfare programs throughout the supply chain.

Key Certifications for Sustainable Manufacturing

Certifications provide third-party verification of a manufacturer's sustainability claims. Here are the most important certifications to look for when sourcing from India:

GOTS (Global Organic Textile Standard)

The world's leading textile processing standard for organic fibers. It covers the entire supply chain from harvesting to labeling, ensuring ecological and social criteria are met. GOTS-certified products must contain a minimum of 70% organic natural fibers.

OEKO-TEX Standard 100

Tests for harmful substances in textiles at every stage of production. Products certified under OEKO-TEX are verified to be free from over 100 harmful chemicals, making them safe for direct skin contact — especially important for children's wear.

BSCI (Business Social Compliance Initiative)

A supply chain management system that helps companies monitor and improve social performance across their supply chains. BSCI audits cover worker rights, fair remuneration, health and safety, environmental protection, and ethical business behavior.

GRS (Global Recycled Standard)

Verifies recycled content in products and tracks recycled raw materials through the supply chain. Essential for brands using recycled polyester, nylon, or blended fabrics that incorporate post-consumer waste.

SEDEX (Supplier Ethical Data Exchange)

One of the world's largest collaborative platforms for sharing responsible sourcing data on supply chains. SEDEX members conduct SMETA audits covering labor standards, health & safety, environment, and business ethics.

India's Advantage in Sustainable Manufacturing

India holds several natural advantages that make it an ideal hub for sustainable apparel manufacturing:

  • World's Largest Cotton Producer: India produces over 6 million tonnes of cotton annually, with growing acreage under organic and BCI cotton cultivation. States like Gujarat, Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, and Telangana lead organic cotton farming.
  • Rich Handloom Heritage: India's traditional handloom sector inherently uses low-energy, artisanal production methods. Khadi, hand-block printing, and natural dyeing are experiencing a global resurgence.
  • Government Support: Schemes like the PLI (Production Linked Incentive) for textiles, Amended Technology Upgradation Fund Scheme (ATUFS), and the National Technical Textiles Mission are incentivizing sustainable manufacturing practices.
  • Cost-Competitive Labor: India offers skilled labor at competitive rates while increasingly enforcing fair wage standards, making ethical manufacturing economically viable.
  • Growing Solar Adoption: Many Indian textile clusters, especially in Tamil Nadu and Gujarat, are rapidly adopting solar energy, reducing the carbon footprint of manufacturing operations.

Sustainable Practices in Printing & Finishing

Beyond fabric choice, the printing and finishing processes significantly impact a garment's environmental footprint. Modern Indian manufacturers are adopting:

  • Water-Based Inks: Unlike plastisol inks that contain PVC and phthalates, water-based inks are biodegradable and produce softer prints with lower environmental impact.
  • DTG (Direct to Garment) Printing: Digital printing technology that eliminates the need for screens and significantly reduces water and ink waste compared to traditional screen printing.
  • Natural & AZO-Free Dyes: Using plant-based dyes and ensuring all synthetic dyes are AZO-free (free from carcinogenic aromatic amines) protects both workers and end consumers.
  • Low-Temperature Finishing: Energy-efficient finishing processes that operate at lower temperatures, reducing energy consumption by up to 30% compared to conventional methods.

The Future of Sustainable Apparel in India

Looking ahead to 2026 and beyond, several trends will shape sustainable apparel manufacturing in India:

  • Circular Fashion: Take-back programs, garment recycling, and upcycling will become mainstream, with manufacturers offering end-of-life solutions for their products.
  • Blockchain Traceability: Supply chain transparency through blockchain will allow consumers to trace every garment back to its raw material source.
  • Bio-Based Materials: Innovations in mushroom leather, algae-based fabrics, and agricultural waste fibers will create new sustainable material options.
  • AI-Driven Efficiency: Artificial intelligence will optimize production planning, reduce overproduction, and minimize energy consumption in manufacturing.
  • Regulatory Push: India's Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) framework for textiles and global regulations like the EU's sustainable textile strategy will enforce higher standards.

Conclusion

Sustainable and ethical apparel manufacturing is no longer a niche — it's a business imperative. India, with its massive cotton production, skilled workforce, growing certification infrastructure, and competitive pricing, is perfectly positioned to be the world's preferred destination for sustainable apparel sourcing.

Whether you're a startup launching your first collection, a college committee ordering custom merchandise, or an established brand expanding your supplier base, choosing a manufacturer committed to sustainability isn't just good for the planet — it's good for your brand's reputation and bottom line.
